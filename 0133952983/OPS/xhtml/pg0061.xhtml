Quiz Questions

  1. You are talking with someone from a different culture who is not very good at remembering long lists of random words or numbers, but who can recite from memory all of his ancestors going back hundreds of years. What is the most likely explanation for this difference in memory skills?

    1. The values and customs of a given culture have a profound effect on what people remember.

    2. The person’s autobiographical memory is stronger than his semantic memory.

    3. The list of his ancestors has been stored in flashbulb memory.

    4. The list of his ancestors is an example of a “recovered memory.”

  2. Your mother is reminiscing about your first birthday party and asks you, “Do you remember when Aunt Mary dropped her piece of cake in your lap?” Try as you might, you can’t recall that incident. This is most likely an example of

    1. memory decay.

    2. retrograde amnesia.

    3. childhood or infantile amnesia.

    4. proactive interference.

  3. If some has a vivid memory of a specific event that occurred during their lifetime, and it includes the circumstances and situation they were in when the event happened, it is referred to as a

    1. flashbulb memory.

    2. extraordinary memory.

    3. eidetic imagery.

    4. autobiographical memory.

  4. Eyewitness testimony is

    1. the only concrete and factual information a jury can absolutely consider accurate in a criminal case.

    2. sometimes inaccurate due to source error.

    3. rarely influenced by events that take place after the initial observation of a crime.

    4. should be considered highly reliable, especially if the witness can recall the event with great confidence.

  5. During psychoanalysis, Alexandra recalls her uncle sexually abusing her during a summer vacation. Prior to this, she had no such memory of the event. This would be considered an example of

    1. source memory.

    2. recurrent memory.

    3. recovered memory.

    4. flashbulb memory.
