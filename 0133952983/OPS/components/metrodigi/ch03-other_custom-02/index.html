Advances in experimental procedures and technology have led scientists to draw many conclusions about the functional organization of the brain. The brain is divided into two hemispheres (left and right) which are connected by the corpus callosum. Two functions that are clearly lateralized (associated with one side or the other) are language and spatial skills (Corina, et al., 1992; Peterson & Fiez, 1993; Bradshaw & Nettleton, 1981). Well-designed and controlled studies consistently find that the left hemisphere is superior in language function while the right hemisphere is superior in spatial relationships.

Much of what we know about the functioning of the two hemispheres comes from research conducted on split-brain patients. The "split-brain" procedure is a surgical technique used for patients who have a seizure disorder and for whom anti-convulsant medications do not help to stop the seizures in the brain. The procedure involves separating the two hemispheres of the brain by severing the corpus callosum.

The corpus callosum is a cluster of several nerve fibers that connect the left and right hemispheres, allowing each hemisphere to send information to the other. Severing the corpus callosum can reduce the likelihood that a seizure will spread from one hemisphere to the other.

However, one effect of severing the corpus collosum is that now the two hemispheres can no longer send information back and forth to each other. Importantly, information in the right hemisphere (which does not process language) can no longer be sent to the left hemisphere (which does). This means that split-brain patients have trouble verbally naming an object if only their right hemisphere sees it.

How does a person “see” an object in only one hemisphere? To answer that, you must understand how the eyes send information to each hemisphere.

The following experiment will test the language abilities of the two hemispheres of the brain.

Students will be presented with a group of letters, or letter string to the left or right side of a flashing dot and asked to determine if it represents a real word or if it is a nonsense combination of letters.

Exposing the two hemispheres to a language test in this way can help determine if the left hemisphere is dominant in language function by being more accurate or faster at this linguistic task than the right hemisphere.

          Some people have very high, even perfect, accuracy in both hemispheres on this task. For people with an intact corpus collosum this simply means that the information in the right hemisphere did travel over to the left hemisphere for language processing. However, that process takes time and, as a result, responses to stimuli presented to the right hemisphere are usually slower than left hemisphere responses. In Figure 2, did you find that your right hemisphere was slower (the taller the bar, the slower the reaction time) than your left hemisphere? If so, this supports the idea that you process language on the left side of your brain.

          If you are like most people, you found that your left hemisphere was more accurate and/or faster at this linguistic task than your right hemisphere. However, not everyone produces those results. You might be one of those extraordinary people who do not demonstrate traditional lateralization of their hemispheres. While this is contrary to what scientists observe in the larger population, it points out to us the importance of knowing that in any population there are individual differences. Specifically, about 20% of left-handed individuals process language primarily in their right hemisphere instead of their left (Taylor et. al., 1990).

          Experiments like the one you just completed, as well as other research techniques and neurological imaging studies have demonstrated that, for most people, language processing is handled by the left hemisphere. An important consequence of this is that people who suffer damage to the left hemisphere, via a stroke for example, often have difficulty understanding or producing language. Similar damage to the right hemisphere often leaves language intact.

          Hemispheric Lateralization is an area in science that will continue to make new discoveries. You should be encouraged to follow the research in this area as scientists uncover more and more information on how the brain works.
