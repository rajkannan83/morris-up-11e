Chances are great that your data replicate a well known phenomenon in memory research called the serial position effect.

In general terms, the serial position effect is that people tend to have better memory for items at the beginning and end of a list than they do for items in the middle of the list.

Click or tap Next to continue.

In the graph of your results, you can see how your memory for the different items depended on the position of the item in the list. Remember that each list had 12 items and you went through 5 lists. Each bar ranges from 0 to 5 and represents how many of the 5 items in that position you recalled.

What most people discover is that they had better memory for the first few items and the last couple of items than they did for the items in the middle of the list. This is known as the Serial Position Effect.

Click or tap Next to continue.

There are two characteristics of memory that come together to create the serial position effect. The first is the primacy effect. Quite simply, the primacy effect is the idea that items at the beginning of the list are more easily remembered. In your results, if the sum of bars 1 through 4 is greater than the sum of bars 5 through 8, then you showed the primacy effect.

The second characteristic is the recency effect. The recency effect is the idea that remembering items at the end of the list is easier. If the sum of bars 9 through 12 is greater than the sum of bars 5 through 8 then you showed the recency effect.

Click or tap Next to continue.

So what causes the serial position effect? A potential explanation for the primacy effect is that the early items got rehearsed more than the middle items. This occurs when someone simply repeats the items as many times as they can. Importantly, the first few items get extra rehearsal because there aren’t many items to remember yet. By the time you get to the middle items, each item only gets a little rehearsal. The extra rehearsal that the first few items get helps move them into long term memory where you have a better chance of recalling them.

The recency effect is explained differently. The last few items, like the middle items, also get very little rehearsal. However, in the recall task you are able to recall the words in any order you like. Many participants choose to write down first the most recent items they saw (the items in the last few positions) while those items are still fresh in their short term memory. Because the recall task occurs immediately after the presentation of the last item, remembering that item requires no rehearsal at all.

Finally, it is important to note that the serial position effect is influenced by how you rehearse the items. Some people, when given a task like this, use imagery and association to learn the list. For example, instead of mentally saying "fly, fly, fly, book, fly, book, fly, book, fly, book, grape...." to rehearse, they instead mentally picture a fly when they see that word. When "book" appears they associate the two by imagining themselves swatting the fly with a book. Then they imagine opening the book to see a picture of grapes. Using an imagery and association method should lead to better recall than using rote rehearsal, and would not necessarily lead to a serial position effect.
